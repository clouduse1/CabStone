How Moisture Testing Works for Concrete Slabs and Wood Subfloors


By the Editorial Team | Published: July 2026 | Last Updated: July 2026

The Number That Decides Installation Day

More floors are destroyed by invisible water than by any visible accident. Concrete slabs emit water vapor for months or years after placement; wood subfloors ride the seasons up and down in moisture content; and every finish floor laid over the wrong number becomes a slow-motion claim - cupped boards, debonded planks, adhesive turned to soup, mold under a vapor-tight vinyl sheet. Professional flooring work therefore begins with instruments, not opinions: moisture is measured, recorded, and compared against the finish manufacturer's published limits before a single plank or tile is committed.

Why Concrete and Wood Are Tested Differently

The two substrates hold water differently. Concrete contains water by chemistry - the mix water that didn't react remains in the slab's pores and migrates upward for a long, slow drying curve measured in months per inch of thickness. Wood holds water by sponge behavior - its moisture content tracks the humidity around it, rising and falling seasonally. Concrete testing therefore asks "how much vapor is still coming out?", while wood testing asks "what is the moisture content right now, and does it match the flooring going over it?"

Testing Concrete Slabs

Relative humidity probes (in-slab RH)

The modern standard (formalized as ASTM F2170) drills small holes to 40 percent of slab depth, seats sensor sleeves, and reads the relative humidity inside the concrete after equilibration. Because the probe reads deep in the slab, it forecasts what the surface will see after flooring seals it. Many resilient and wood flooring products publish limits in the 75 to 80 percent RH range, with some adhesives and coatings rated higher.

Calcium chloride tests (MVER)

The older surface method (ASTM F1869) seals a dish of calcium chloride salt under a plastic dome for about three days and weighs the moisture it gains, reporting a moisture vapor emission rate in pounds per 1,000 square feet per 24 hours. Common limits fall around 3 to 5 pounds depending on the finish. It reads only the surface's current behavior - which is why the deeper RH method has become the primary specification tool, with MVER often run alongside.

What happens when a slab fails

Failing numbers don't cancel projects; they change them. Options include more drying time with dehumidification, moisture-mitigating epoxy coatings that cap the slab, vapor-retarding underlayments rated for the measured level, or switching to finishes tolerant of the reading. What passing numbers buy is the manufacturer's warranty standing behind the floor.

Testing Wood Subfloors

Wood is read with a moisture meter - pin types that measure electrical resistance between two probes driven into the panel, or pinless types that scan with an electromagnetic field. Readings are taken across the whole floor, not one spot, because plumbing walls, exterior corners, and crawlspace zones tell different stories.

Two numbers matter. The first is the subfloor's absolute reading: framing lumber and panels should generally be at or below the low teens in percent moisture content before finish floors go down - decks read high after construction wetting and need drying time. The second is the differential: solid hardwood specifications commonly require the subfloor and the flooring to be within about 4 percentage points of each other (tighter, around 2 points, for wide planks). A large gap means the two will move toward each other after installation - and movement after installation is what cupping, gapping, and buckling are made of.

Acclimation: The Part Everyone Rushes

Acclimation is the practice of letting flooring reach equilibrium with the home's actual living conditions before installation - with the HVAC running, because a house at construction humidity is not the house the floor will live in. The often-skipped truth is that acclimation is verified with the meter, not the calendar: "three days in the room" means nothing if the readings haven't converged. Materials differ, too - solid hardwood demands it, many rigid vinyl products specify temperature stabilization instead, and each manufacturer's sheet outranks folklore. All of it belongs to the same discipline of measured, verified subfloor preparation that separates floors that last from floors that get replaced.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does a new concrete slab take to dry?

The rule of thumb is roughly a month per inch of thickness under decent drying conditions - so a 4-inch slab needs months, not weeks, and cool, humid, or unventilated conditions stretch it further. Only testing tells you when it's actually ready.

Can flooring go over a slab that tests wet?

Often yes, with mitigation: epoxy moisture barriers, vapor-retarding underlayments rated for the reading, or finish products tolerant of higher RH. The unacceptable option is installing a moisture-sensitive floor over a failing number and hoping - that's how adhesives re-emulsify and planks debond.

What moisture content should a wood subfloor be before installing hardwood?

Generally at or below the low teens in percent moisture content, and within about 4 percentage points of the hardwood being installed (about 2 points for wide planks). Both numbers come off a meter on installation day, not from assumption.

What does cupping tell you about moisture?

Cupped boards - edges high, centers low - mean the board bottoms are wetter than their tops, water arriving from below. The fix starts under the floor (slab vapor, crawlspace humidity, a leak), not with sanding the cup flat, which backfires when the boards later dry and crown.

Do vapor barriers under flooring replace testing?

No. Barriers and retarders are rated for specific emission levels, and picking the right one requires the measurement. Over a severely wet slab, an ordinary pad-with-film underlayment simply traps the problem under a warranty-voided floor.

Is a quick pinless meter scan enough for a whole house?

Scanning is the right way to cover area fast, but suspicious zones deserve pin readings for depth and confirmation. The professional pattern is scan everywhere, pin-verify the outliers, and record everything - documentation is what stands behind the installation later.

Why did a floating floor fail over a slab that "felt dry"?

Because feel reads surface temperature, not vapor. A slab can present a bone-dry surface while pushing pounds of vapor daily into anything sealed over it. Instruments exist precisely because hands and eyes cannot measure what destroys floors.
