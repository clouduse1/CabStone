Plywood vs. OSB Subflooring: How the Two Panels Compare


By the Editorial Team | Published: July 2026 | Last Updated: July 2026

Two Panels, One Job, Different Personalities

Walk any framing lumberyard and the subfloor aisle offers two products: plywood and oriented strand board. Both are engineered wood panels, both carry span ratings from the same certification system, and both build millions of floors a year. Yet they behave differently enough - especially around water - that the choice still generates debate among builders three decades after OSB became the volume leader. Understanding how each panel is made explains nearly everything about how each performs.

How Each Panel Is Made

Plywood is built from thin veneers peeled from logs, stacked in alternating grain directions, and bonded under heat and pressure. The cross-graining is the genius of the product: wood is strong along its grain and weak across it, so alternating layers cancel each other's weaknesses and resist swelling in both directions.

OSB starts as wood strands - thin rectangular flakes - blended with wax and resin, formed into thick oriented mats (surface strands aligned with the panel's length, core strands crossing them), and pressed into panels. Because strands can come from small, fast-growing trees and nearly the whole log becomes product, OSB uses resources efficiently - the root of its consistent price advantage.

The pattern in the table has one theme: OSB is the more uniform and economical panel, plywood the more forgiving one when water enters the story. Neither is "cheap" or "premium" by nature - both are certified structural products - but they fail differently, and floors are one of the places failure shows.

The Edge-Swell Question

The famous OSB weakness is edge swell. When panel edges take on water - a rainy framing stage, a plumbing leak, damp concrete below - OSB edges absorb, thicken, and hold much of that thickness after drying. Under forgiving finishes like carpet, a swollen seam is invisible; under hardwood, laminate, or vinyl plank, it telegraphs as a ridge line the flooring installer must grind flat. Plywood swells too, but it dries out faster and returns closer to its original dimension.

Manufacturers answered with enhanced OSB subfloor products - denser, more resin, edge sealants, and warranties measured in months of weather exposure rather than days. These premium panels have largely closed the practical gap on protected sites, which is why the modern decision often reduces to schedule and exposure: a fast-dried-in build tolerates standard panels; a slab of winter framing argues for enhanced OSB or plywood.

What the Ratings Actually Say

Every legitimate panel carries a certification stamp declaring its span rating (the joist spacing it's engineered to cross), its exposure class (Exposure 1 panels tolerate construction-phase weather, not permanent wetting), and its end use. The stamp outranks the brand debate: a 23/32-inch tongue-and-groove panel rated Sturd-I-Floor 24 on joists 16 inches on center performs, whichever material it is. Where floors go wrong is panels installed off-rating - sheathing grades used as single-layer subfloor, or spans stretched past the stamp - failures the plywood-versus-OSB argument gets blamed for but didn't cause.

Installation Outweighs the Panel Choice

Both products squeak, hump, and flex identically when installed badly. The practices that decide the outcome are the same for both: subfloor adhesive on every joist before the panel lands, ring-shank nails or screws on the specified schedule, the 1/8-inch gap at panel ends and edges (enhanced tongue-and-groove profiles self-gap), panels acclimated and dry before finish floors trap them, and swollen edges sanded before installation day. A glued-and-screwed OSB deck outperforms a nailed-only plywood deck every time - the verb matters more than the noun. How those preparation steps translate into a floor that lasts is exactly the ground covered by dedicated subfloor preparation practice.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is plywood stronger than OSB?

At the same span rating, no - both are certified to carry the same loads, and OSB's uniform core is actually more consistent panel to panel. Plywood's advantages are moisture recovery and slightly better fastener holding, not raw strength.

Why do builders use OSB if plywood handles water better?

Cost and consistency. OSB runs meaningfully cheaper per panel with steadier pricing, has no core voids, and performs identically in the dry, protected conditions a finished house provides. On a build that gets dried-in promptly, the moisture difference rarely gets a chance to matter.

What is a tongue-and-groove subfloor panel?

A panel whose long edges interlock - a protruding tongue on one side, a groove on the other - so adjacent panels share loads between joists. It eliminates the need for blocking under edge seams and is the standard for single-layer floor decks.

Can OSB be used under ceramic tile?

Yes, within the tile industry's methods - typically with a backer board or uncoupling membrane over it rather than tile bonded directly. Several methods once written for plywood now include OSB; what never changes is the deflection and flatness the tile requires beneath.

What does "Exposure 1" mean on a panel stamp?

The panel's glue bond tolerates temporary construction-phase weather but the panel is not rated for permanent exterior or wet-area exposure. Nearly all subfloor panels are Exposure 1 - they survive the framing stage, not a chronic leak.

How long can a subfloor stay rained on during construction?

Standard panels are designed to shrug off a normally paced construction wetting, and enhanced subfloor products carry explicit no-sand warranties for exposure windows commonly ranging from 200 days to a year. The practical rule: dry the deck, check edges with a straightedge, and sand any swelling before finish floors arrive.

Should a squeaky floor be blamed on the panel type?

Almost never. Squeaks come from movement at fasteners and seams - nailed-only installation, missed joists, skipped adhesive - and both panel types squeak equally under those conditions. The cure is refastening, not re-paneling.
