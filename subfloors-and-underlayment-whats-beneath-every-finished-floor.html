Subfloors and Underlayment: What's Beneath Every Finished Floor

By the Editorial Team | Published: July 2026 | Last Updated: July 2026

The Floor You See Is Only the Top Layer

Every finished floor - hardwood, tile, vinyl plank, carpet - is the visible surface of a layered system, and the layers nobody sees determine how the visible one performs. A floor system typically stacks four elements: the structural joists, the subfloor panels fastened across them, an underlayment matched to the finish material, and the flooring itself. When a floor fails early - cupping boards, cracked grout, peaking laminate seams, squeaks that arrive with the first heating season - the diagnosis almost always lives in those hidden layers rather than in the product that shows. The craft of getting those layers right before any finish flooring goes down is its own discipline, laid out in Subfloor Preparation: The Hidden Work That Determines Floor Lifespan. This guide maps the system those preparations serve.

Layer One: Structure - Joists and Deflection

The subfloor can only be as good as what carries it. Joist spacing, span, and stiffness determine how much a floor flexes underfoot, and flex is measured as deflection - expressed in ratios like L/360, meaning the span divided by 360. Ceramic tile tolerates no more than L/360 before grout and tile begin to crack; natural stone demands roughly twice that stiffness, near L/720. Wood and resilient floors are more forgiving, which is why a floor that carried carpet without complaint can crack tile within a year of a remodel.

Structure also sets the fastening grid. Panels are engineered to span specific joist spacings - the ubiquitous 16-inches-on-center, or 19.2 and 24 inches in some framing systems - and a panel rated for one spacing installed over a wider one will feel spongy no matter what goes on top.

Layer Two: The Subfloor - Panels That Do the Carrying

The subfloor is the structural deck: plywood or oriented strand board (OSB), commonly 23/32-inch tongue-and-groove panels, glued and fastened across the joists. Its job is stiffness, fastener holding, and a continuous surface. The glue matters as much as the fasteners - a glued-and-screwed deck resists the seasonal movement that produces squeaks, while a nailed-only deck almost guarantees them as wood shrinks around nail shanks over the first few years.

Panel edges tell their own story: tongue-and-groove profiles lock neighboring panels so an edge under load transfers weight to its neighbor instead of deflecting alone, and the small expansion gaps specified between panels exist because wood-based panels grow and shrink with moisture. Panels butted tight in a humid summer buckle in place; panels gapped correctly move without drama.

Underlayment is whatever the specific finish floor requires between itself and the subfloor, and it changes completely by material:

The recurring mistake is treating underlayment as generic padding. Each finish material fails in its own way - tile cracks, vinyl telegraphs, hardwood squeaks against its fasteners - and each underlayment exists to prevent that specific failure. Substituting across categories converts a warranty into a story.

Layer Four: The Conditions - Moisture and Flatness

Two site conditions govern whether the stack above succeeds. The first is moisture: wood-based panels and finish floors move with their moisture content, and concrete slabs emit water vapor for months or years. Floors are installed to numbers, not to feel - moisture meters for wood, humidity probes or surface tests for concrete - because a floor installed over a wet substrate is a claim being scheduled. The second is flatness: most flooring specifications allow no more than about 3/16 inch of deviation over a 10-foot straightedge. Flatness is not levelness - a uniformly sloped old floor can be perfectly flat - and it is corrected mechanically by sanding high spots and filling lows with patch or self-leveling compounds before anything is installed.

Reading a Floor Problem Back Through the Layers

The system view turns floor complaints into diagnoses. Cupped hardwood points to a moisture imbalance below; cracked grout lines point to deflection or a skipped uncoupling layer; a wave in vinyl plank points to flatness never corrected; seasonal squeaks point to fastener withdrawal in the deck. The finish floor is usually the witness, not the culprit - and the repairs that last are the ones that fix the layer that actually failed.

What is the difference between a subfloor and an underlayment?

The subfloor is the structural deck fastened to the joists - it carries loads. Underlayment is the non-structural layer between subfloor and finish floor, chosen to suit the specific flooring above it. One house has one subfloor; it may host several different underlayments room by room.

How thick should a subfloor be?

The common standard for joists 16 inches on center is 23/32-inch (nominal 3/4-inch) tongue-and-groove panels. Wider joist spacing or stone tile calls for thicker panels or an added layer - stiffness, not thickness alone, is the real requirement.

Can new flooring go over old flooring?

Sometimes - a floating floor over well-adhered sheet vinyl is routine, while anything over cushioned or loose material is not. The questions are always the same: is the existing layer flat, dry, solidly attached, and low enough that added height won't trap doors and appliances? Any "no" means removal.

What does an uncoupling membrane do under tile?

It separates tile from the wood structure so each can move independently - the polyethylene layer absorbs the substrate's seasonal movement instead of transmitting it into grout and tile. Over wood subfloors, membranes or backer board are the difference between a tile floor and a future crack map.

Why do floors need expansion gaps at walls?

Wood and wood-based floors expand and contract with humidity across the seasons. The perimeter gap - hidden under baseboard - gives that movement somewhere to go. Floors installed tight to the walls buckle upward in the first humid summer, a failure that looks dramatic and was entirely preventable.

How flat does a floor need to be for new flooring?

Most manufacturers specify about 3/16 inch maximum deviation over 10 feet (some rigid products tighten this). A straightedge and feeler check before installation is the whole test - and correcting a failed spot costs a fraction of replacing a failed floor.

Does a concrete slab need different preparation than a wood subfloor?

Yes, fundamentally. Concrete is tested for vapor emission and residual moisture, treated with vapor barriers or moisture-mitigating coatings, and ground or leveled mechanically. Wood decks are checked with a moisture meter, refastened, and repaired panel by panel. Same goals - dry, flat, solid - reached by different routes.
